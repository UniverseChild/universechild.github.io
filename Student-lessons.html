What Surrealism Is

  • Surrealism began in Europe after World War I and aimed to blend dream and reality into a new "super-reality"
  • Artists were inspired by dreams, imagination, and the subconscious, often using surprise, strange juxtapositions, and illogical scenes
  • Common themes include dreams and nightmares, memory, childhood, desire, and mysterious symbolic objects

Middle School Surrealism Lesson: "Dream World Mash-up"

Goal: Students create a "dream scene" by combining real objects, animals, and places in impossible, imaginative ways.

1. Hook and Introduction (1 class)

  • Show 3-4 kid-friendly surreal images and ask: "What feels normal? What feels weird?"
  • Explain: Surrealism = real things + impossible combinations, like in dreams

2. Core Surrealist Ideas

  • Juxtaposition â€“ putting things together that don't belong
  • Transformation â€“ changing something into something else
  • Scale â€“ changing sizes (tiny elephant on a giant pencil)

3. Brainstorming Game: "What if...?"

Give students a 3-column sheet:

  • Column A: Places (beach, forest, bedroom, city street)
  • Column B: Objects/animals (clock, chair, cat, apple)
  • Column C: Weird changes (melting, floating, tiny, giant)

4. Planning: Thumbnail Sketches

Students draw 3 small sketches of different ways to arrange their surreal idea, then choose their favorite.

5. Drawing the Scene (1-2 classes)

Step-by-step:

  1. Lightly sketch the normal parts first
  2. Add the surreal parts (moved objects, changed sizes, transformations)
  3. Check: At least one strange focal point? Two surreal tricks used?

6. Adding Color and Mood

  • Warm colors (reds, oranges, yellows) = energetic or strange
  • Cool colors (blues, purples, greens) = dreamy or calm
  • Choose a main color mood to match the feeling

7. Details and Finishing Touches

Add small details: tiny doors, floating umbrellas, mismatched shadows. Leave some calm, empty areas to make the weird parts stand out.

8. Reflection and Share Time

  • Gallery walk: students view each other's work silently
  • Peer feedback: "I notice...", "I wonder...", "This feels like a dream because..."
  • Written reflection about their surreal idea and creative choices

Key Surrealist Techniques for Middle Schoolers

Core "Idea" Techniques

  • Unexpected juxtapositions: Put unrelated objects together in one scene
  • Transformation: Turn one thing into another
  • Scale changes: Make tiny things huge and huge things tiny
  • Symbolism: Use repeated personal symbols for feelings and memories

Automatic and Chance-Based Techniques

  • Automatic drawing: Draw without planning, then find creatures in the marks
  • Exquisite corpse: Group draws sections on folded paper; unfold to reveal wild figure
  • Frottage: Rub crayons over textured surfaces, turn patterns into surreal landscapes

Paint and Texture Play

  • Paint blots: Press painted papers together, add details to mirrored blobs
  • Collage: Cut and recombine magazine photos into impossible scenes
  • Double imagery: Design shapes that read as two things at once
